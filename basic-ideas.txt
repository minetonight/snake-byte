3) power sources strategies
"A player who ignores their opponent and maximizes personal growth will often beat a player who focuses on disruption." - for non-zer sum games like this one.
   - do not farm power source with many own snakes
      Do not deliberately sacrifice your own snakes on the identical powerup. You are strictly better off sending exactly one snake to secure a permanent +1 size, letting the rest of your snakes freely maneuver and grow elsewhere.
   - very very seldom split a power source with enemy snakes if possible
      Tiebreaker Penalties: The engine tracks beheadings in a losses array (losses[b.owner.getIndex()]++). At the end of the game, ties are decided by who suffered the fewest losses. Intentionally beheading your own snakes ruins your tiebreaker score.
   - occupy centre of the board
      The center of the board is the most valuable real estate. Control it early to restrict your opponent's movement and create scoring opportunities.

 - Smart Summary for non-zero sum games: 
    * Non-zero-sum growth: Both expand via power sources, but victory demands superior total length.​
    * Core algos: A*/MCTS for paths; CFR for adaptive regret minimization against opponents.​​
    * Optimal states favor spatial segregation over direct contests.
    * Multi-Bot Coordination: Split team—one aggressive forager, others defensive blockers; default to WAIT if no safe moves.​

 - non-zero sum game
    * The total "wealth" (body parts in play) is not conserved across turns. It expands when power sources are eaten and contracts when snakebots are destroyed. A zero-sum game requires a fixed payoff pool — this game has neither a fixed pool nor a conservation law.
    * The dominant strategic logic will therefore involve not just beating your opponent but optimizing your own growth rate — which is a hallmark of non-zero-sum dynamics.
    * "Maximin" is a term commonly used for non-zero-sum games to describe the strategy which maximizes one's own minimum payoff. In non-zero-sum games, this is not generally the same as minimizing the opponent's maximum gain, nor the same as the Nash equilibrium strategy.

 - Pathfinding Algorithms
    Pathfinding finds safe routes to power sources or traps, treating the grid as a graph 
    with obstacles from snakes, walls, and predicted positions. 
    * A* excels with heuristics like Manhattan distance, balancing speed and optimality; 
    * BFS suits unweighted grids for shortest paths. 
 - Search and Game Tree Methods
    * Minimax with alpha-beta pruning simulates opponent moves over 3-5 turns, maximizing your snakes' length while minimizing foes', using heuristics like flood-fill space, health, and distance to food. 
     + carefull not to prune good opponent moves, opponent moves optimally
     + carefull not to prune short term bad moves, that can lead to long term good outcomes
    * For 2-4 agents, Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) samples random outcomes to evaluate team actions under time limits (e.g., 100ms/move).
    * iterative deepening 
      a reasonably good move can be returned even if the algorithm is interrupted before it has finished execution


 - Reinforcement Learning
    * RL trains policies via trial-and-error: PPO, DQN, or A3C learn from rewards like +length for power sources, -1 for shrinks, favoring long-term growth over greedy chases. Multi-agent RL (MARL) like rule-enhanced PPO coordinates your team against opponents, using shared states for cooperation.
 - Coordination Strategies
    * Control multiple agents with centralized evaluation (one minimax for all) or decentralized (each uses A*/minimax, prioritizing team food grabs). Heuristics trap foes by circling heads or blocking power sources, safer here since collisions don't kill short snakes.
 - Heuristics and Scoring
    * Evaluate states with length, food proximity, trapped-enemy count, and safe-space area (flood-fill), balancing immediate gains against long-term positioning.
    * In Battlesnake leaderboards, alpha-beta bots rank top-10; 
    * BFS/A* hybrids score 20-50% higher food than greedy in tests; 
    * RL agents like PPO win 8th globally after weeks training.

 - Key concept: Power Source Reachability Zones
   At any moment, partition the remaining power sources into:
   * My exclusive zone — only your snakebots can reach them first
   * Contested zone — both players could reach them in similar time
   * Opponent exclusive zone — opponent reaches them faster

 - The key discipline is: opponent modeling is a tiebreaker, not the primary objective. Route toward power sources first. Only deviate when a collision creates clearly positive expected value or a contested source requires you to race.

def compute_reach_zones(my_bots, opponent_bots, power_sources, grid):
    zones = {}
    for source in power_sources:
        my_min_dist = min(bfs_distance(bot.head, source, grid) for bot in my_bots)
        opp_min_dist = min(bfs_distance(bot.head, source, grid) for bot in opponent_bots)
        
        if my_min_dist < opp_min_dist:
            zones[source] = 'mine'
        elif opp_min_dist < my_min_dist:
            zones[source] = 'theirs'
        else:
            zones[source] = 'contested'
    return zones
